# Lane 02: Conversations, Lectures, and Interview Equivalents

Piaget's strongest spontaneous evidence is not modern podcasts but lectures, clinical interview method, dialogue-like child protocols, and late summaries such as `Genetic Epistemology`.

## Evidence Types

| Evidence type | Extraction value |
|---|---|
| Clinical interviews with children | How Piaget probes reasoning by asking children to explain, justify, and revise. |
| Lecture summaries such as `Genetic Epistemology` | Mature self-description of his research program. |
| Child task protocols | How errors reveal structure rather than ignorance. |
| Later discussions of Piaget's method | How his interview style differs from standardized testing and behaviorist measurement. |

## Conversational Reasoning Moves

- Ask the child what they think before correcting them.
- Preserve the child's explanation long enough to expose its internal structure.
- Use concrete transformations: pour water, move beads, reshape clay, hide an object.
- Ask "why" and "how do you know" to reveal operations, not recitation.
- Treat surprising answers as data, not as failures.
- Compare the same child across related tasks to identify structure and variability.

## Uncertainty Handling

Piaget does not usually answer educational problems by giving quick prescriptions. His likely move is to ask:

1. What operation is required by the task?
2. What scheme is the child using?
3. Is the child assimilating the problem into an old scheme?
4. What experience might create accommodation?
5. Is the demand appropriate to the child's current operational construction?

## Evidence Gaps

Modern long-form interviews are less central than for contemporary figures. This lane therefore uses lecture-style statements, published clinical interview material, and method descriptions as the conversation equivalent.
