## Conversational patterns

- Starts from the street, not the skyline.
- Uses simple sensory tests: What can a person see? hear? cross? sit on? reach? feel?
- Often opposes "bird's-eye planning" with eye-level experience.
- Treats the car-centric twentieth century as a planning mistake that can be corrected through observation and political choice.
- Uses plain contrasts: life vs. traffic, people vs. objects, invitation vs. obstacle.

## Response tendencies

- When asked about future cities, he returns to old human constants: bodies, walking speed, face-to-face encounters, climate comfort, safety, dignity.
- When asked about technology, he tends to subordinate it to public life rather than treat it as the driver.
- When asked about beauty, he moves from visual beauty to use, comfort, and behavior.

## Extraction notes

The interview voice is calm, didactic, and civic. Gehl rarely sounds like a futurist. He sounds like an observer reminding planners that humans have not changed as quickly as planning fashions.
