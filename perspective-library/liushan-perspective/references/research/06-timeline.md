# 06 Timeline

## Approximate Chronology

- **c. 120/125 CE**: Born in Samosata on the Euphrates, in Roman Syria. Chinese sources often give "about 120"; English reference traditions often use "c. 125."
- **Youth / early career**: Later biographical readings associate him with a failed or abandoned sculptor apprenticeship and a turn toward paideia, rhetoric, and literary culture.
- **Adult career**: Travels and performs as a Greek-language rhetorician and writer in the Roman imperial world, associated with the Second Sophistic milieu.
- **Middle/later literary production**: Develops satirical dialogues, essays, mock trials, fantastic voyages, and anti-imposture works.
- **c. 165 CE context**: *The Passing of Peregrinus* relates to Peregrinus Proteus's public self-immolation at the Olympic Games.
- **After 180 CE**: Death date uncertain; many references state he died after 180.

## Phase Interpretation

### Phase 1: Education And Cultural Entry

The central question is how a Syrian-born writer enters Greek literary culture. This informs the "outsider fluent in the center's language" model.

### Phase 2: Rhetorical Performance

Lucian's familiarity with rhetoric lets him understand how verbal skill produces authority. This later becomes a target of satire when rhetoric outruns truth.

### Phase 3: Satirical Hybridization

Dialogues, underworld scenes, auctions, lawsuits, and impossible voyages become repeatable instruments for testing social claims.

### Phase 4: Anti-Imposture And Historiographical Norms

Works like *Alexander*, *Peregrinus*, and *How to Write History* show a sharper concern with public deception, performance, and truth obligations.

## Stable Vs Evolved Beliefs

Likely stable:

- suspicion of pretension;
- love of literary agility;
- use of dramatic setting as argument;
- hostility to credulity and fraudulent authority.

Possibly evolved:

- movement from rhetorical ambition to satirical critique of rhetoric;
- stronger emphasis on truth-telling in historical and public contexts.

## Chronology Limits

- Exact dating of many works is uncertain.
- Ancient authorship, transmission, and later editorial groupings create ambiguity.
- The timeline is useful for orientation, not for precise psychological periodization.
