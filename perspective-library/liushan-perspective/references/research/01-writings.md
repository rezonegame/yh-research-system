# 01 Writings

## Evidence Base

Primary texts sampled for the operational model:

- *True History / A True Story*: explicit fictional travel narrative that mocks writers who present impossible tales as true.
- *Dialogues of the Dead*: status-stripping conversations in the underworld.
- *Dialogues of the Gods*: mythological burlesque that lowers divine authority into petty dialogue.
- *Philosophies for Sale*: philosophical schools staged as goods in a slave-market/auction.
- *The Way to Write History / How to Write History*: polemic against panegyric, florid irrelevance, and servility in historical writing.
- *Alexander the False Prophet*: satirical biography of a religious entrepreneur and fraudster.
- *The Passing of Peregrinus*: satire of public self-dramatization, Cynic performance, and fame-seeking martyr posture.
- *The Fisherman*: defensive sequel that clarifies the attack is against false philosophers rather than philosophy as such.

## Recurrent Claims And Moves

1. **Truth needs enemies**: false historians, fake prophets, pretentious philosophers, and miracle-mongers are recurring targets.
2. **The setting is an argument**: moving gods to dialogue, philosophers to auction, and the rich to the underworld does much of the reasoning work.
3. **Satire can be epistemic**: ridicule is not decoration; it is a diagnostic instrument for claims that survive only under solemn lighting.
4. **Fiction can expose false realism**: *True History* tells overt lies to make covert literary and historiographical lies visible.
5. **Status is a costume**: death, market exchange, and ordinary questioning strip titles from people and doctrines.
6. **Style is part of the fraud ecology**: excessive rhetoric, learned display, and mysterious language are often how weak claims buy authority.

## Favorite Examples

- The moon voyage and whale interior in *True History*: impossible events narrated with sober detail.
- Auctioning Pythagoreanism, Socraticism, and other creeds in *Philosophies for Sale*: thought systems as goods with pitches.
- Rich and famous dead losing their earthly advantages in *Dialogues of the Dead*.
- Alexander's oracle enterprise and Glycon cult in *Alexander*: fraud as staged technology plus audience desire.
- Peregrinus's public death performance: philosophy as spectacle and reputation management.

## Extracted Principles

- If a claim demands reverence before evidence, remove the reverence and see what remains.
- If a story is suspiciously vivid, ask whether detail is being used as proof.
- If a doctrine is sold as salvation, inspect the seller, buyer, price, and after-sales consequences.
- If a historian praises power too eagerly, the text has become panegyric.

## Evidence Gaps

- Authorship and dating across the Lucianic corpus are not equally secure.
- The skill uses translated texts; fine-grained Greek style claims should be treated as lower confidence unless checked against Greek.
- The surviving corpus is rich but still mediated by manuscript transmission and later editorial decisions.
