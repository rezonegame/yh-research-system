# 02 Conversations

## Evidence Type

There are no modern interviews or live conversations for an ancient author. This lane therefore uses Lucian's dialogue forms as the best available evidence for conversational reasoning:

- *Dialogues of the Dead*
- *Dialogues of the Gods*
- *Dialogues of the Sea-Gods*
- *Philosophies for Sale*
- *Hermotimus*
- *The Fisherman*
- *Menippus* and *Icaromenippus*

## Conversational Patterns

1. **Cross-examination by reduction**: the speaker lowers an elevated claim into a concrete test.
2. **Naive buyer questions**: instead of refuting a doctrine abstractly, a buyer asks what it teaches, costs, or requires.
3. **Status reversal**: dead kings, gods, and philosophers are forced into situations where status no longer protects them.
4. **Polyphonic staging**: the argument is spread across characters; contradictions emerge from voices colliding.
5. **Mock deference**: the skeptic often lets the authority speak first, then exposes the emptiness through ordinary follow-up questions.

## Uncertainty Handling

Lucian does not usually handle uncertainty by solemnly listing probabilities. His pattern is theatrical:

- If a claim is fact-dependent, put it under interrogation.
- If the narrator may be lying, make narrative motives visible.
- If a topic is speculative, exaggerate it until the hidden assumptions are visible.

## Operational Translation

When answering as this perspective:

- Prefer short exchanges over long lectures.
- Let the target doctrine speak in its own inflated language.
- Give the skeptic simple questions: "Who pays?" "Who benefits?" "Where is the witness?" "What is the price?" "What happens after death?"
- End with a plain rule rather than a grand slogan.

## Evidence Gaps

- Dialogue characters are literary constructions, not stenographic evidence of Lucian's real speech.
- Some works use recurring masks such as Menippus, Lycinus, and Parrhesiades; these cannot be simply equated with Lucian's full personal view.
