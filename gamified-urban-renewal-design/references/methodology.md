# Methodology Reference

This reference distills the local chunk corpus under `C:\AgentTest\Anything2Ontology\output\chunks` into a reusable method for gamified urban renewal and urban design.

## Core Synthesis

Gamified urban renewal works when the game is a translation layer between everyday experience and professional planning. The game should lower the threshold for expression, make trade-offs visible, and create documented evidence that can be evaluated by planners, residents, officials, and operators.

v0.2 strengthens this synthesis with one additional rule: a project is not complete until its evidence, game mechanics, design translation, operation model, and release label are aligned. A beautiful route or workshop is still a draft if official boundaries, resident consent, merchant burden, or maintenance ownership remain unresolved.

The local corpus points to five foundations:

1. Serious games in spatial planning: games help with experimentation, engagement, collaboration, and complexity, but risk inconsistency, oversimplification, distrust, and cost.
2. Block by Block/Minecraft: a robust participatory workflow moves from planning, to co-design workshop, to expert translation and implementation.
3. Low-cost digital twin/game-engine workflow: data layer, design layer, co-creation game layer, and pilot/evaluation layer.
4. Historic Urban Landscape and heritage-led regeneration: interventions must integrate tangible and intangible heritage, social values, governance, finance, and sustainable development.
5. Analogue and playable-city systems: board/map/card/role-play tools can be more inclusive and easier to adapt than expensive digital simulations.

## Method Stack

### Layer 1: Urban Renewal Constraint Layer

Define the problem before designing the game.

- Physical: site boundary, morphology, infrastructure, open space, green/blue systems.
- Social: daily users, residents, merchants, vulnerable groups, trust, conflict, memory.
- Cultural: tangible heritage, intangible practices, authenticity, identity, tourism pressure.
- Economic: ownership, local enterprise, funding, operating model.
- Governance: permissions, responsible bodies, maintenance, regulatory constraints.

This layer prevents the game from becoming a free-floating idea session.

### Layer 2: Participation Layer

Use participation as a designed system.

- Identify stakeholders and representative sample.
- Separate powerful decision-makers from under-represented voices without excluding either.
- Provide consent, civility, safety, accessibility, and data rules.
- Use site walks, observation prompts, memory mapping, and value cards before asking for solutions.
- Use debriefing to make hidden assumptions explicit.

### Layer 3: Game Layer

Choose the least complicated game form that can answer the planning question.

Useful mechanics:

- placement/removal: tests spatial priorities and land-use trade-offs;
- budget tokens: reveals resource constraints;
- persona/role cards: exposes differing user needs;
- value cards: surfaces cultural, safety, ecological, and social priorities;
- event/shock cards: tests resilience and operational uncertainty;
- voting/prioritization: ranks ideas but must not replace feasibility review;
- scenario rounds: compares alternative futures;
- debrief prompts: captures reasons, not just choices.

Avoid mechanics that reward winning over understanding. Competition is useful only when it reveals real tension, such as budget scarcity or conflicting mobility needs.

### Layer 4: Translation Layer

Every game output must become a planning input.

- Heat of repeated placement -> program demand or spatial node.
- Conflicting placement/removal -> contested value or governance issue.
- Persona dissatisfaction -> equity risk.
- Popular but infeasible proposal -> design alternative or phased pilot.
- Narrative/story from participants -> intangible heritage and identity cue.
- Scenario failure -> operational or resilience gap.

The translation layer should produce matrices, maps, annotated photos, intervention lists, and policy/design notes.

### Layer 5: Implementation Layer

Close the loop.

- Expert design studio turns co-created ideas into feasible options.
- Validation meeting checks whether the translation preserved community intent.
- Phasing separates quick wins, pilots, capital works, and governance reforms.
- Monitoring measures both spatial outcomes and participation outcomes.

## Recommended Complete Workflow

1. Scoping and commitment
   - Confirm site, authority, budget logic, and whether implementation is realistic.
   - If there is no implementation path, frame the project as research/exploration rather than promising change.

2. Evidence and baseline
   - Collect maps, photos, policy documents, ownership, heritage constraints, mobility/access data, user observations.
   - For low-cost digital twins, consider open GIS, OSM, DEM/DTM, satellite imagery, Blender/OSM, and game engines only when they answer a real decision need.

3. Stakeholder recruitment
   - Map users, affected groups, decision-makers, technical experts, operators, and funders.
   - Define representative sample and inclusion support.

4. Pre-workshop learning
   - Use site walk, questionnaire, story/memory prompts, and values cards.
   - Teach the game with a low-stakes exercise.

5. Co-design gameplay
   - Let groups build, place, remove, negotiate, and explain.
   - Facilitators document decisions, arguments, alliances, conflicts, and emotional reactions.

6. Presentation and prioritization
   - Groups present their designs.
   - Participants prioritize not only preferred elements but reasons, trade-offs, and conditions for acceptance.

7. Expert translation
   - Convert game outputs into spatial strategy, design alternatives, budget bands, and implementation phasing.
   - Preserve minority concerns and conflict points.

8. Validation and revision
   - Return translated options to participants and decision-makers.
   - Ask whether the translation is faithful, feasible, and fair.

9. Implementation and monitoring
   - Define quick wins, pilots, capital projects, governance/maintenance tasks, and evaluation indicators.

## Game Form Selection

### Analogue Board/Map Game

Best when the group has mixed digital literacy, little budget, or the project needs fast iteration. Use real maps for site-specific work and abstract boards when the goal is to reveal preferences without overloading participants.

Design needs:

- board/grid/map;
- service or intervention pieces;
- budget/resource constraints;
- persona or stakeholder cards;
- action rules;
- satisfaction and reflection sheet.

### Minecraft/Voxel Co-Design

Best when participants need to express spatial ideas visually and collaboratively without architectural drawing skills. It is especially strong for youth engagement, public spaces, and under-represented groups.

Design needs:

- site selection and permission;
- 3D/Minecraft model;
- hardware, software, facilitation;
- opening session, site walk, shared vision, training, building, presentation, prioritization;
- expert design studio and validation.

### Digital Twin/Game-Engine Walkthrough

Best when evaluating spatial scenarios, visibility, shade, access, or immersive public understanding. Use it when the project can support data preparation and privacy management.

Design needs:

- data layer;
- 3D reconstruction;
- game-engine import;
- navigable demo;
- feedback collection;
- iteration loop.

### Role-Play Negotiation Game

Best for governance, conflict, funding, land use, mobility, tourism pressure, or heritage trade-offs. Use carefully so roles do not stereotype vulnerable groups.

Design needs:

- role cards with goals and constraints;
- shared project board;
- negotiation rounds;
- event cards;
- agreement template;
- debrief.

## Evaluation Logic

Evaluate before, during, and after.

Before:

- participant profile;
- baseline knowledge;
- trust and expectations;
- site concerns;
- accessibility needs.

During:

- engagement;
- dominance and silence;
- collaboration;
- conflicts;
- rule comprehension;
- design decisions;
- facilitator interventions.

After:

- satisfaction;
- perceived influence;
- learning;
- trust;
- feasibility perception;
- priority list;
- translation accuracy after expert review.

## Heritage and Culture Gate

For historic blocks and culture-led regeneration, require:

- mapping of tangible and intangible heritage;
- distinction between resident value and visitor value;
- authenticity and setting review;
- protection of everyday life from over-tourism;
- adaptive reuse logic;
- local enterprise and benefit-sharing;
- governance and maintenance;
- interpretation that does not flatten living culture into decoration.

## Cultural-Tourism Activation Layer

Use this layer when the user asks for 文旅, cultural tourism, cultural routes, visitor experience, local IP, cultural activation, night economy, heritage interpretation, or culture-led regeneration.

The core question is not "how do we attract tourists?" but "how can visitors, residents, local businesses, and heritage values coexist through a playable and governable experience?"

### Required Analysis

- Locality: What stories, crafts, foodways, rituals, industries, landscapes, memories, and everyday practices are specific to this place?
- Audience: Who are the intended visitors, and what can they responsibly learn, do, buy, or contribute?
- Resident benefit: Which local groups gain income, recognition, services, or participation power?
- Carrying capacity: What levels of visitor flow, noise, queuing, parking, waste, and night activity can the area absorb?
- Authenticity: Which elements should be interpreted, protected, adapted, or kept private?
- Operations: Who runs routes, games, events, shops, guides, maintenance, safety, and data collection?
- Seasonality: What changes between weekday/weekend, day/night, peak/off-season?

### Game-Based Cultural Tourism Methods

- Heritage quest: visitors collect stories or solve place-based tasks, but the tasks must teach real spatial/cultural knowledge.
- Memory map: residents add stories, names, lost uses, and valued places before any tourist route is designed.
- Route-building game: participants assemble routes under time, accessibility, heritage, commercial, and crowd constraints.
- Business ecosystem game: merchants, residents, operators, and officials negotiate product mix, opening hours, event rules, and revenue logic.
- Carrying-capacity scenario game: players respond to crowding, noise, weather, waste, traffic, and heritage wear.
- Interpretation card game: local stories are sorted into public, guided, sensitive, commercial, and protected categories.

### Cultural-Tourism Output Requirements

For 文旅 projects, produce:

1. Place narrative system: themes, sub-stories, evidence, local speakers, and what not to commercialize.
2. Visitor journey: arrival, orientation, route, pause points, participation tasks, consumption, reflection, exit.
3. Experience products: game/workshop/event formats, route cards, map tasks, guided scripts, digital or analogue tools.
4. Local enterprise linkage: residents, merchants, makers, guides, schools, museums, cultural groups.
5. Operations model: staffing, schedule, ticket/free logic, maintenance, safety, sanitation, data/privacy.
6. Carrying-capacity and ethics plan: crowd, noise, traffic, heritage wear, displacement, privacy, sacred/private spaces.
7. Benefit-sharing and evaluation: local income, resident satisfaction, visitor learning, repeatability, environmental load.

### Anti-Generic Checks

Reject or revise any文旅 idea that:

- could be copied to any town by changing the name;
- depends mainly on photo spots, slogans, or generic IP mascots;
- uses residents as scenery rather than partners;
- increases visitor traffic without maintenance and safety capacity;
- monetizes heritage without identifying who benefits;
- turns private memory, sacred practice, or vulnerable community life into spectacle.

## Common Failure Modes

- Over-gamification: points or badges replace real deliberation.
- Tokenism: residents play but decisions are already fixed.
- Oversimplification: the game hides budget, ownership, regulation, or heritage constraints.
- Data theatre: a digital twin looks impressive but does not inform decisions.
- Persona substitution: imagined users replace reachable participants.
- Weak translation: game results are photographed but not converted into design action.
- No closure: workshop creates expectations without funding, phasing, or governance.

## Quality Repair Patterns

If research rigor is weak:

- add a source basis and cite the method family;
- separate evidence from assumptions.

If participation integrity is weak:

- revise stakeholder sampling;
- add inclusion supports and consent;
- reduce expert dominance.

If game design is weak:

- clarify planning question;
- reduce rules;
- add meaningful constraints;
- strengthen debrief.

If implementation is weak:

- add ownership, phasing, budget bands, and maintenance logic.

If deliverability is weak:

- split outputs into classified documents and a final integrated report.
